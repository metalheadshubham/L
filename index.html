Research Paper on Digital Warfare

Author: L Lawliet

From January 9, 2025, I began tracking the digital footprint of some of the world’s most dangerous terrorist groups. These groups, operating under the radar, have increasingly shifted their focus to the digital realm, using cutting-edge technology to fund their operations, recruit fighters, and move resources without leaving a trace. The connections between Al-Qaeda, ISIS, Hezbollah, and Hamas have deepened through an unseen network—one that operates through encrypted communications, Bitcoin transactions, and a decentralized infrastructure. This is the world of the Haqquine Network (HN), a dark web of digital connections that I attempted to infiltrate and expose.

On January 15, 2025, I stumbled upon Khalil Faris, a figure who remains behind the scenes but whose influence stretches across multiple terror organizations. While not a front-line fighter, Khalil acts as a central coordinator, ensuring that ISIS receives the financial backing it needs to continue its brutal operations, that Hezbollah stays armed despite the mounting pressure in Lebanon, and that Hamas maintains its strength even as the Gaza Strip is repeatedly targeted by Israel. Through his management of the Haqquine Network, Khalil ensures that no matter how much money is seized or how many fighters are captured, these groups remain well-funded and operational.

The Haqquine Network operates with precision, leveraging a digital ecosystem that ensures anonymity for its users. At its core, the network relies on Bitcoin, using decentralized digital wallets and blockchain technology to move funds across borders without a single trace. The key to its success is its ability to stay ahead of law enforcement and intelligence agencies by constantly changing its tactics and communication methods.

By January 25, 2025, I had started to unravel the recruitment strategy being employed by Samira al-Fayez, a senior recruiter for Hezbollah. Her role is pivotal in bringing in new blood, particularly vulnerable young recruits who are then groomed for jihadist causes. Through encrypted platforms, she communicates with individuals, enticing them with promises of belonging and purpose. These recruits are given a unique Bitcoin wallet, which acts as their ticket into the network—a means of receiving training, supplies, and, ultimately, a new life as a combatant for one of these terror groups.

One of the more disturbing revelations came when I traced several Bitcoin transactions flowing into Gaza on February 1, 2025. Rather than focusing on the rebuilding efforts after Israeli airstrikes, a significant portion of the funds was being used to procure weapons and to train new fighters. This money, which came directly from the Haqquine Network, ensured that Hamas could maintain its operations, keeping its militants armed and ready for the next round of violence. Khalil Faris and his network remain the silent facilitators of this perpetual cycle of violence.

The recruitment strategy of these organizations has evolved to keep pace with the digital age. Rather than relying solely on physical propaganda or traditional methods of indoctrination, these groups use social media, encrypted messaging apps, and the dark web to find and recruit new members. On February 5, 2025, I intercepted a conversation between Samira al-Fayez and a potential recruit from Europe, who was convinced that jihad was his calling after weeks of online interaction. Samira utilized every tactic to manipulate him—targeting his vulnerabilities, promising him purpose, and making him feel part of a much larger, righteous cause.

The use of Bitcoin in recruitment goes far beyond just financial transactions. Recruits are given wallets that act as proof of their commitment to the cause. This method ensures that recruits are fully integrated into the network, making it easier for them to be mobilized at a moment’s notice, whether for fighting or for conducting operations.

On February 10, 2025, I thought I was close to a breakthrough. I managed to track one of Khalil’s transactions back to a digital wallet located in a remote server in Eastern Europe. I knew that if I could expose this, I might be able to disrupt the flow of funds. But just as quickly as I had found the wallet, it disappeared. The encryption was unbreakable, and the wallet had already been shifted to another address. This constant cat-and-mouse game was frustrating—every time I thought I had a clear path, the network adapted.

The digital nature of this war made it impossible to fight with the traditional methods of counter-terrorism. Law enforcement agencies were too slow to understand the significance of Bitcoin, too focused on physical tracking and human intelligence to keep up with the speed and agility of these digital networks.

By February 13, 2025, it became clear to me that the EU and other international organizations were struggling to catch up. Despite some efforts to monitor the dark web and combat money laundering, they were failing to address the real issue: the ability of these groups to operate beyond the reach of traditional surveillance. The EU’s response to the rise of digital terrorism had been sluggish, with bureaucratic hurdles making it difficult for member states to coordinate effectively. As a result, figures like Khalil Faris continue to thrive, coordinating between various terror groups without fear of reprisal.

The events in Gaza, the rising tension in Lebanon, and the global struggle to understand the true nature of digital terrorism all point to one undeniable conclusion: the war is changing. No longer confined to the physical world, terrorism is now a digital entity, one that moves through encrypted networks and uses cryptocurrencies to fuel its operations. Without a more aggressive global response to these methods, organizations like Al-Qaeda, ISIS, and Hamas will continue to evolve, operating in the shadows while governments scramble to catch up.
